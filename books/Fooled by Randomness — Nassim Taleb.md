# Fooled by Randomness — Nassim Taleb

**On distinguishing signal from noise, and why most "successful" traders are just lucky.**

## Why Read This

This book rewires how you think about success, skill, and randomness in markets. Taleb's central thesis: humans are systematically terrible at distinguishing skill from luck, and the financial industry is built on this confusion.

Read this before you start attributing your backtest results to skill. Read it again after your first drawdown.

## Key Takeaways

- **Survivorship bias.** You see the winners, never the graveyard. For every successful trader, there are hundreds of blown-up accounts you never hear about. The visible survivors look skilled; they may just be lucky.
- **Alternative histories.** Don't judge a decision by its outcome. Judge it by the full distribution of possible outcomes. A reckless bet that pays off is still a bad decision. Russian roulette winners are not skilled.
- **Randomness is underestimated.** Random processes generate far more apparent structure than people expect. Streaks, trends, and patterns in random data fool humans (and algorithms) constantly. See [[Overfitting]].
- **Skewness matters.** A strategy that makes money 99% of the time and blows up 1% of the time looks great until the 1% event hits. Taleb calls this "picking up nickels in front of a steamroller."
- **Emotional resilience.** Even knowing about randomness intellectually doesn't prevent the emotional pain of drawdowns. The practical solution: don't look at your P&L too frequently. Higher sampling frequency = more noise = more suffering.
- **[[Fat Tails]] are the norm.** Real-world distributions are not Gaussian. Extreme events are far more common than standard models predict. Build your risk management for the real distribution, not the convenient one.
