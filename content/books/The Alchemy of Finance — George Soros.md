**Reflexivity — prices affect fundamentals and vice versa. Macro thinking from one of the greatest traders.**

## Why Read This

Soros made billions by thinking about markets differently from academics. While efficient market theory says prices reflect fundamentals, Soros argues the relationship is circular: prices influence fundamentals, which in turn influence prices. This feedback loop — **reflexivity** — is the core of his framework.

The book is part philosophy, part real-time trading diary. The diary sections are fascinating: Soros documents his actual trades and reasoning during the mid-1980s, including mistakes. It's raw and honest in a way most trading books aren't.

## Key Takeaways

- **Reflexivity.** Markets are not passive mirrors of reality. They actively shape reality. A rising stock price improves a company's ability to raise capital, which improves fundamentals, which justifies the higher price — until it doesn't. The same feedback loop works in reverse during crashes.
- **Boom-bust model.** Reflexive processes create self-reinforcing trends that eventually become unsustainable. The pattern: initially justified trend → reflexive amplification → speculative excess → critical point → reversal → reflexive collapse. Applies to crypto cycles viscerally.
- **Fallibility.** All market participants operate with imperfect understanding. This isn't a bug — it's the fundamental condition. Models are always wrong; the question is whether they're usefully wrong.
- **The human element.** Soros trades based on "feel" as much as analysis. He famously said his back pain told him when positions were wrong. The book doesn't teach a replicable system — it teaches a way of thinking about macro relationships.
- **Macro as reflexive system.** Currency markets, credit markets, and regulatory cycles are all reflexive. Understanding the feedback loops is more valuable than understanding the "fundamentals" in isolation.
