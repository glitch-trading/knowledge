## What It Is

The study of how cognitive biases and emotional states distort trading decisions, and the process discipline used to keep execution aligned with the pre-defined plan rather than with whatever the trader is feeling in the moment.

**Recurring failure modes:**

- **Freezing at the level**: price reaches the planned entry and the trader does not pull the trigger because they now believe it will keep moving.
- **Plan deviation under stress**: moving stops, widening targets, sizing up after losses to "make it back".
- **Round-tripping winners**: holding through a target out of greed, watching it reverse.
- **Revenge trading**: re-entering immediately after a loss without a fresh setup.
- **Overconfidence after a win streak**: sizing up beyond the strategy's expectancy.
- **Confirmation bias**: only seeing the confluences that support the trade you want to take.

The asymmetry: emotions are not removable. Even disciplined traders feel a spike at execution. The skill is *acting on the process rather than on the emotion*, not pretending the emotion isn't there.

## Why It Matters

Psychology is where a positive-expectancy system is silently destroyed. The journal will show that the strategy was profitable in theory and unprofitable in execution — the gap is psychological.

**Process-based mitigations:**

- **Articulated entry / stop reasoning**: writing the "Why?" for entry and stop placement before execution makes deviation harder to rationalize after the fact. See [[Trade Journaling]].
- **Pre-mortem / devil's advocate**: actively list reasons *not* to take the trade. Demoting a perceived A* setup to a B is far cheaper than discovering it during the trade.
- **TP limit orders**: pre-place exits so the decision is made when calm, not when watching tape.
- **No post-execution adjustment rule**: do not move TP after the trade is on. Removes the most common round-tripping mechanism.
- **Emotion log during the trade lifecycle**: capture what you felt at entry, mid-trade, and exit — not just what you did. The root cause of a freeze or a deviation lives in this data, not in the chart.
- **Lifecycle-stage attribution**: when reviewing a mistake, identify whether it was an entry, management, or exit failure. The fix is stage-specific.
- **Process-based goals, not P&L goals**: the trader controls process; the market controls P&L. Goals like "always use TP limit orders" or "journal every trade" are achievable; "make $X this week" is not.

For systematic strategies the equivalent failure mode is the *operator* — turning off the bot in a drawdown, re-tuning parameters mid-run, intervening on individual trades. The same discipline applies: act on the process the system was built to follow, not on the emotion of the moment.
